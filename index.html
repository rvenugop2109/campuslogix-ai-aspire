Pillar 01 of 05
Think Clearly — Logical Reasoning and Structured Problem-Solving
01
Pillar 1 · The Foundation

The skill
no automation
can replace.

Logical reasoning is not a module in this programme. It is the spine — the thread woven through every session of every year. In Grade 6 it is taught explicitly. By Grade 9 it is how students think.

Why it matters — for parents
Every competitive entrance exam — JEE, NEET, CLAT, CAT, UPSC — has a logical reasoning component. This programme builds it from age 11, through practice, not by accident.
What students can do by Grade 9
🔍
Recognise and apply patterns
Identify rules, sequences, and structures across unfamiliar situations — not just familiar ones.
↔️
Reason with conditional logic
Construct and evaluate if-then arguments. Identify exactly where a chain of reasoning breaks down.
🧩
Decompose complex problems
Break any large, messy problem into structured, solvable parts before attempting a solution.
⚖️
Distinguish evidence from opinion
Evaluate a claim by the quality of its evidence — not by how confidently it is stated.
🛡️
Identify logical fallacies
Name at least 8 common fallacies and spot them in real AI-related arguments, news, and advertising.
🔄
Steel-man an opposing view
Construct the strongest possible version of an argument they disagree with — before critiquing it.

Pillar 02 of 05
Understand the Landscape — AI Literacy and Conceptual Fluency
02
Pillar 2 · AI Literacy

Not just what
AI does — but
how it works.

Students who only know what AI produces are passive users. Students who understand how it learns, where it fails, and what it cannot do are active, critical participants — in every field, at every level.

Why it matters — for parents
AI is being discussed in every boardroom, every policy brief, and every family dinner table. A student who genuinely understands what AI is will navigate the next decade with clarity while others navigate it with anxiety.
What students can do by Grade 9
🧠
Explain how AI learns
Describe training data, model accuracy, and the difference between supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning — without technical jargon.
🗺️
Distinguish types of AI
Narrow vs general AI, generative vs predictive AI, the difference between a model, a tool, and an application.
📊
Understand data as AI's raw material
Why data quality, quantity, and bias determine what an AI can and cannot do — and what garbage in, garbage out really means.
🇮🇳
Identify AI in Indian daily life
Recognise AI across healthcare, agriculture, finance, education, and law enforcement — with specific Indian examples and context.
⚠️
Understand AI's limits
Hallucination, bias, context blindness — and why each of these matters in high-stakes decisions.
📰
Read AI news critically
Evaluate any AI claim — from a news article, product pitch, or policy brief — for accuracy, exaggeration, or misleading framing.

Pillar 03 of 05
Work With AI — Prompt Engineering and AI Collaboration
03
Pillar 3 · Prompt Engineering

The practical
skill of our time.

Prompt engineering is already listed in job descriptions across every industry. More importantly, the underlying skill — communicating precisely and iterating intelligently — is valued in every profession, every institution, and every collaborative environment.

The four-year progression
Grade 6: clear instructions · Grade 7: RCT framework · Grade 8: chain prompting and output shaping · Grade 9: system prompts, few-shot, and meta-prompting
What students can do by Grade 9
✍️
Write precise, effective instructions
Communicate with AI clearly enough that the output is useful on the first or second attempt — not the tenth.
🎯
Apply the RCT Framework
Construct any prompt using Role + Context + Task as a default mental model, across every subject and every tool.
🔗
Design and execute prompt chains
Break a multi-step project into a sequence of prompts — each building on the last — and document the logic as a flowchart.
⚙️
Write and deploy system prompts
Set persistent behaviour for an AI session. Build a specialised AI assistant for a specific purpose and test its consistency.
🔁
Apply meta-prompting
Ask AI to critique and improve their own prompts — then evaluate whether the AI's suggestion is actually better, and why.
📚
Maintain a personal Prompt Toolkit
A documented, reusable set of prompts covering every tool they use — with usage notes, examples, and known limitations.

Pillar 04 of 05
Build With AI — Vibe Coding and AI-Assisted Creation
04
Pillar 4 · Vibe Coding

From idea to
working product.
No syntax needed.

The ability to build a working digital product was once the exclusive domain of computer science graduates. Vibe coding changes that. Students describe what they want in plain English — the AI builds it — and the student evaluates, iterates, and ships.

Why it matters — for parents
This is not a coding course. It is a creation course. A student who leaves school able to prototype an idea and iterate on feedback has a capability that most adults in the workforce do not yet have.
What students can do by Grade 9
🏗️
Prototype from plain-English descriptions
Build functional multi-page web applications using Lovable — describe it, it builds it — without memorising syntax.
🗺️
Design before building
Produce a full solution architecture — system map, feature plan, AI integration brief — before opening any build tool.
🐛
Debug with structured reasoning
Diagnose why something is not working, write a precise problem statement, and direct the AI to fix it — without touching code directly.
🎙️
Produce AI voice and video
Generate ElevenLabs voiceovers and Canva Video AI components as part of a complete multi-modal project.
🎓
Present and defend their work
Explain what they built, why they built it, what they would improve — to a live panel that includes external guests.
📁
Maintain a Build Portfolio
A body of work spanning Grade 8 and 9 — school-useful app, team Mini-Project, and individual Capstone.

Pillar 05 of 05
Think Responsibly — AI Ethics, Digital Citizenship, and Responsible Use
05
Pillar 5 · AI Ethics

The pillar that
makes all the
others safe.

Teaching students to use AI without teaching them about bias, misinformation, deepfakes, and accountability is like teaching them to drive without road safety. Ethics is not a final chapter in this programme — it is woven through every single grade.

The four-year progression
Grade 6: can AI be wrong? · Grade 7: deepfakes and misinformation · Grade 8: who is responsible when AI fails? · Grade 9: design responsible AI from the brief up
What students can do by Grade 9
🎯
Identify bias in AI systems
Explain how biased training data produces biased outputs — and why this matters in hiring, lending, healthcare, and law enforcement.
🔒
Protect their own data
Apply consistent data hygiene: what to share, what never to share, how to identify when a tool is harvesting data they did not intend to give.
🕵️
Recognise and resist misinformation
Spot hallucinated content, deepfakes, and AI-generated disinformation. Verify claims before sharing or acting on them.
⛓️
Map accountability chains
When AI causes harm, trace the chain of decisions — developer, company, regulator, buyer, end user — and identify where responsibility sits.
🏛️
Understand their rights
Know what the DPDP Act means personally. Understand that their data has value and that they have rights over it.
🔧
Design responsible AI from the start
Write an AI solution brief with fairness safeguards built in — not retrofitted as an afterthought. Conduct a full ethical review before building.

Tool Introduction Approach
In Grade 6, tools are introduced slowly and carefully — observation before participation. Students watch the teacher use the tool for a full session before touching it themselves. The first student hands-on session is always in pairs, with the teacher circulating. No student works on a tool alone in Grade 6.
Compliance Checkpoint
School-managed ChatGPT Edu and Claude for Education accounts must be active and tested before Week 18. Parental consent forms must be signed and on file. Teacher must complete a dry-run session independently before Week 18.
Data Hygiene Rule — Established in Grade 6
From the first tool session: "No real names, no personal details, no school information in any prompt — ever." This rule is taught as a skill, not a restriction, and carries through all four grades.

The Landscape Approach — How Every Tool Category Is Introduced
20·40
The Pedagogy

See all options first.
Choose based on merit.

Every tool category is introduced through a 20-minute teacher-led landscape demo before any student creates an account. Students observe three to four tools from the same category, doing the same task, side by side. Then they choose their primary tool — and log their reasoning.

🕐
20 min landscape · 40 min primary
The session structure for every tool introduction across all four grades
🧠
Students build judgment, not habits
Seeing the landscape first prevents tool lock-in — students understand why they chose, not just what they use
📋
Every choice is documented
Tool Choice Log maintained from Grade 7 — submitted as a Capstone artefact in Grade 9
How It Works — Every Tool Category Introduction
1
Landscape Demo — Teacher-Led
Teacher opens 3–4 tools from the same category with identical briefs. Students observe output quality, interface, and trade-offs. No student accounts yet.
2
Compare & Discuss — Class Dialogue
Which tool produced the best output for this brief? Which was fastest? Which gave the most control? Students form and defend their own view before a primary tool is named.
3
Primary Tool Named — Hands-On Begins
The programme recommends a primary tool for each category. Students then spend 40 minutes using it — first in pairs, then independently as confidence builds.
4
Tool Choice Log Updated
Students document the category, the tools demoed, the primary tool chosen, and their own reasoned justification. This entry becomes part of their Grade 9 Capstone Design Package.
The Tool Choice Log
A three-year running document — started in Grade 7, updated at every landscape demo, submitted in Grade 9. It is the evidence that a student's tool decisions were deliberate, reasoned, and self-directed — not just whatever the teacher last demonstrated.
